Automation & Shopfloor Systems

Moving from manual data collection to near-real-time production visibility.

SCADA MES OPC-UA OEE Data Historian Dashboard

01 — Context

Industrial production plant where shopfloor data was collected manually. Operators recorded output counts, downtime events and quality results in paper forms or end-of-shift spreadsheet entries. Management reporting was delayed by at least 24 hours and depended on data entry accuracy that varied significantly between shifts.

The plant had a SCADA system in place for equipment monitoring, but its data was not being extracted for production management purposes.

02 — Problem

Decisions were made on stale data. When a production shortfall occurred, it was only visible at end of shift or the following morning — too late to intervene. Downtime events were categorised inconsistently because operators assigned them manually without a defined taxonomy. No comparison of actual production rate against target was visible during the shift.

The consequence was reactive management: problems were investigated after they had impacted delivery, not while there was still time to recover.

03 — Approach

The project proceeded in phases:

  • Data availability review: mapping the existing SCADA data points (OPC tags) against the information required for production tracking. Counter signals, machine states, alarm events.
  • Gap analysis: identifying what was available but not extracted, what required additional instrumentation, and what required process definition (downtime taxonomy, shift definitions).
  • Requirements workshop: structured sessions with production supervisors, maintenance and quality to define what visibility they actually needed — rather than what the technology could theoretically provide.
  • Architecture design: data flow from SCADA historian to reporting layer, transformation logic for OEE calculation, dashboard structure.
  • Prototype: Power BI dashboard connected to extracted historian data, reviewed and iterated with end users before finalising.

04 — Tools & Methods

  • SCADA OPC data point mapping
  • OEE framework (Availability × Performance × Quality)
  • Downtime taxonomy definition and standardisation
  • Stakeholder requirements workshops
  • Power BI prototype dashboard
  • Data historian extraction and transformation

05 — Outcome & Learning

The most significant finding was that most of the required data already existed in the SCADA historian. The gap was not technical — it was architectural and process-related. No one had defined what questions the data should answer, and no one had built the extraction path to a usable format.

The requirements workshop was the highest-value activity in the project. It surfaced a significant disagreement between what management thought they needed (granular production counts per order) and what operators needed (real-time rate vs. target per shift). Both were addressed in the final design, but only because the conversation was structured early.

The experience reinforced a general principle: in data projects, the data architecture question should follow the business question, not precede it.

06 — What This Demonstrates

  • OT/IT integration — SCADA to reporting layer
  • SCADA data architecture and OPC data point mapping
  • OEE framework implementation from raw machine signals
  • Requirements definition for operational dashboards
  • Data-driven operations design from a process-first perspective
