Energy & ISO 50001

Energy Management / ISO 50001

Implementing a systematic energy management framework — from energy review to certified management system.

ISO 50001 EnPIs SEUs Energy Baseline CUSUM Internal Audit

01 — Context

Manufacturing plant where energy represented a significant portion of operational costs. The facility had no systematic approach to energy management — consumption was tracked only at utility billing level, with no sub-metering, no baseline and no linkage to production variables.

The project was initiated by a combination of cost pressure and a strategic goal of ISO 50001 certification, which had become a customer requirement in the sector.

02 — Problem

Without a defined energy baseline or Significant Energy Uses (SEUs), there was no structured basis for improvement. Any energy-saving initiative could not be properly evaluated because there was no agreed reference point for what consumption should be under defined operating conditions.

Energy consumption data existed — utility meters, occasional sub-meter readings — but it was not integrated with production data. A month with high consumption might be explained by high output, or it might indicate a genuine inefficiency. There was no way to distinguish between the two.

03 — Approach

The implementation followed the ISO 50001:2018 framework structure:

  • Energy review: mapping all energy inputs, consumption points and energy uses across the facility. Identifying which equipment and processes were the primary consumers.
  • SEU identification: formally identifying Significant Energy Uses — the operations accounting for the majority of consumption and/or with the greatest potential for improvement.
  • Energy baseline establishment: developing regression-based baselines linking energy consumption per SEU to relevant variables (production volume, operating hours, ambient temperature where applicable).
  • EnPI definition: defining Energy Performance Indicators — specific, measurable metrics for tracking performance against the baseline over time.
  • Monitoring plan: designing the data collection infrastructure: sub-metering requirements, data recording frequency, responsibility assignments.
  • Management review process: establishing the governance structure required by the standard — energy team, roles, internal audit cycle, management review agenda.

04 — Tools & Methods

  • ISO 50001:2018 framework and clause requirements
  • Energy review methodology (consumption mapping)
  • SEU identification and ranking criteria
  • Regression-based energy baseline modelling
  • Energy Performance Indicators (EnPIs) design
  • CUSUM tracking for baseline deviation monitoring
  • Energy Action Plans
  • Internal audit protocol

05 — Outcome & Learning

The structured framework replaced a reactive, billing-level review with a systematic management process. SEUs were formally identified, sub-metered and linked to production variables — enabling normalised comparison across periods with different output levels.

An energy management team was formed with defined roles and a regular review cadence. This organisational change was as significant as the technical work: it created accountability and a shared language for energy performance.

The most common mistake encountered in ISO 50001 implementations is treating it as a documentation exercise rather than a management system change. The standard requires evidence of systematic improvement, not just records of consumption. Projects that focus only on the documentation typically fail to sustain the certification after the initial audit.

06 — What This Demonstrates

  • ISO 50001:2018 implementation from zero baseline to certification-ready state
  • Energy management systems design and governance
  • Technical work: baseline modelling, EnPI design, sub-metering architecture
  • Organisational work: team formation, roles, management review process
  • Systematic improvement methodology applied to energy domain
