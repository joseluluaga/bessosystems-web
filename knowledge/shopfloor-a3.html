From Shopfloor Observation to A3 Problem Solving

6 min read · Lean & Industrial Engineering · BESSÓ Systems
A3 Lean Gemba 5-Why PDCA

Why observation beats assumptions

Gemba is a Japanese term meaning "the actual place" — in manufacturing, the shopfloor. A Gemba walk is not an inspection tour. It is the act of going to where value is created to understand how the process actually works, as distinct from how it is documented or assumed to work.

Most process improvement failures can be traced to decisions made without adequate observation. Countermeasures are designed for a problem that was misunderstood, or an analysis is constructed from second-hand data that did not reflect reality. The A3 format is a discipline for avoiding this — but only if it is grounded in direct observation.

Before you go: define the problem space

Arriving at the floor without a defined problem space is common and counterproductive. You will observe many things; without focus you will not observe anything with sufficient depth. Before going to the floor, define:

  • The process boundaries (where does it start, where does it end?)
  • The symptom you are investigating (quality escapes? delays? rework rate?)
  • The metrics you will collect (cycle time, defect count, wait time)
  • Who you will observe with (operator involvement at this stage is critical)

The problem statement does not need to be precise at this point — precision comes from the observation. But it needs to be focused enough to direct your attention.

Observation protocol

During the Gemba walk, your role is to observe and record, not to correct or suggest. Typical observation activities:

  • Time each operation step multiple times (cycle time variability is itself informative)
  • Note where operators deviate from the documented standard — and why
  • Identify queue points: where material or information waits
  • Record what information triggers each step (a physical card? a verbal instruction? a screen?)
  • Ask operators to explain what they do when something goes wrong. Their workarounds reveal the gaps in the formal process.

Photograph the workspace if permitted. Sketches of the physical layout help later when constructing the current state in the A3.

Structuring the A3

An A3 is a single-page problem-solving document, originally formatted on A3 paper to force concision. The six standard sections map directly to the observation cycle:

Background

Why this problem matters now. Context, business impact, scope. Keep it to two or three sentences. If you cannot explain why it matters, reconsider whether it is the right problem to address.

Current State

A factual description of the process as it operates today, based on direct observation. Include the data you collected: cycle times, defect rates, wait times. Use a process map or VSM sketch where it adds clarity. Avoid judgment — describe what you saw.

Target State

What the process should look like once the problem is resolved. Expressed in measurable terms — not "reduce defects" but "reduce defects at station 4 from 3.2% to under 1%." A target state is specific and time-bounded.

Analysis

The causal analysis connecting current state to root cause. Use 5-Why or a fishbone diagram. The depth of the analysis should match the complexity of the problem — a five-step causal chain is more credible than a two-step one for chronic problems. Show your reasoning, not just your conclusion.

Countermeasures

The specific actions that will close the gap between current and target state. Each countermeasure has an owner, a due date, and a measurable outcome. Countermeasures address root causes — not symptoms.

Follow-up

How progress will be monitored. What metric confirms the countermeasure worked? When is the next review? Who is responsible?

"A3 is not a form. It is a thinking discipline. Filling in the boxes without the underlying thinking produces documentation. Doing the thinking first, and then writing it down on one page, produces a problem-solving record you can act on."

Common mistakes

  • Multiple problems in one A3. One A3 per problem. If the scope expands, create a separate A3 or escalate the frame.
  • Current state from memory. The current state must be based on observed data. "We think the cycle time is about 40 seconds" is not a current state.
  • Countermeasures without owners. A countermeasure with no owner is a wish, not a plan.
  • Skipping the follow-up. An A3 that is closed without verifying the outcome contributes nothing to organisational learning.

Practical tips

Write the A3 with the operator who knows the process, not for them. The document should reflect shared understanding, not a management interpretation of the problem. If the operator disagrees with the current state description, the description is wrong.

Resist the impulse to move to countermeasures before the analysis is complete. The pressure to act is constant in production environments. But a countermeasure applied to the wrong cause will not solve the problem — and may introduce a new one.
